# Our research
<img src="images/SEI.svg"> Electrode–electrolyte interphases are thin, protective layers that naturally form where battery electrodes touch the liquid electrolyte. Although only nanometers thick, they play an outsized role in how rechargeable batteries work. These interphases control which ions can pass, prevent unwanted chemical reactions, and keep the electrodes stable during cycling. A good interphase allows lithium or sodium ions to move smoothly while blocking electrons and reactive molecules. A poor one leads to capacity loss, gas formation, and short battery life. Understanding and engineering these interphases is essential for creating safer, longer‑lasting, and higher‑performance rechargeable batteries.
